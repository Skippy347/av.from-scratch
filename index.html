8 Ways to Overcome the Fear of a Blank Page

June 5, 2022

Starting something new can feel intimidating and overwhelming.A short piece of code or a few paragraphs of text — the pressure of beginning can be paralyzing. Before writing anything meaningful, the author of this piece brewed a cup of tea, checked messages, and wandered around the room aimlessly, trying to think of the perfect opening sentence.

At one point, a dusty bookshelf seemed far more urgent than the work at hand, so it was cleaned thoroughly. The tea, forgotten, went cold and had to be replaced with a fresh cup. Meanwhile, the blank page stayed exactly the same, silently demanding action yet offering no hint of where to start.

Turning Creative Anxiety into Action

June 6, 2022

Creative work often begins with hesitation. The cursor blinks, the ideas swirl, and nothing seems ready to put into words. The key is to move past the need for perfection and simply begin — even with a rough, messy draft. Momentum comes from action, not from waiting for inspiration to arrive fully formed.

By lowering the pressure and focusing on progress rather than flawless results, the once daunting blank page becomes an opportunity rather than a threat. Each sentence, however imperfect, is a step forward and proof that the fear can be turned into creative momentum.

Why is a blank page so scary?

A blank page by itself isn’t scary, of course. What we’re usually afraid of is the amount of work that lies ahead— the effort, that mental hurdle we have to overcome to switch the brain from a state of panic into a state of flow. There can be many reasons why taking those first steps feels so hard. Here are the main ones:

  • Uncertainty about the result. And whether it can even be achieved at all. How can you be sure you’re not doing all this work for nothing? That you’ll see it through to the end? And before you start—well, there’s nothing to finish.

  • Fear of criticism. If someone will be evaluating the work, then even in the very early stages we start looking at it through that person’s eyes. We imagine all the flaws they might find. After that, the desire to do anything at all can vanish instantly.

  • Perfectionism. When it seems that if the work won’t be done perfectly, then there’s no point in starting it at all. In such moments we can forget that it’s actually rare for something to turn out great on the very first try.
